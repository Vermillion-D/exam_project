1. Introduction to Ninjas

Ninja (忍者, aka shinobi, meaning "those who act in stealth") were a type of warrior who specialized in unconventional warfare such as infiltration, sabotage and assassination during the age of the samurai. Ninja employed deception and forgery tactics to take opponents out by surprise. Due to their unorthodox methods, which contradicted the way of the warrior (bushido), the ninja were not credited with the same honor as the samurai.

The diverse range of skills practiced by the ninja is called ninjutsu - the art of stealth - and includes training in armed combat, weapon techniques, military strategy, meteorology, geometry and breathing synthesis. Ninjutsu was passed down through generations within families or directly from teacher to a selected one or few disciples. This secrecy in part explains why little factual information exists about the ninja, giving rise to lots of stereotyping of them both within and outside of Japan.

2. Ninjas History

As early as the 11th century, the inhabitants of Iga and Koka gained a reputation for their skills in unconventional warfare. Close to the political center Kyoto, yet distinctly remote due to their rugged terrains, Iga and Koka presented an ideal hideout location for refugees, bandits and losing parties of battles. Many ninja families lived here within autonomously governed communities, developing their martial skills for self-defense.

During the age of the warring states in the 15th and 16th centuries, many warlords would hire the ninja of Iga and Koka as mercenaries for their set of unique skills in facilitating the conquest of castles and victories in battles. These were the heydays of the ninja.

Once peace was established in the 17th century, the ninja were mainly employed by the Tokugawa Shogunate to spy on the various regional lords and to guard Edo Castle. During these times, the ninja became greatly romanticized in popular culture, such as kabuki, which led to inaccuracies in the common perception of them. The stereotypes were subsequently exported to the West and further developed to entertain audiences there.

3. Ninja “Supernatural” Powers

The shinobi were said to have outright supernatural powers such as turning invisible, teleportation, manipulation of the elements, walking on water, and shapeshifting. Of course, these things fall squarely within the realm of fantasy, but the clever use of ninja tools and guerilla tactics helped to create the illusion of supernatural abilities. Shinobi, being masters of misdirection, had it in their best interest to allow such stories to propagate.

Mention is made in some of the older chronicles about the use of mudras, or hand seals, along with mantras, or chants to create magical effects. These were mostly intended for a psychological boost, i.e. to trigger a certain mental state accessed through meditation, but they were referred to as outright magic in the manuals as a form of misdirection. Believing that a shinobi actually possessed supernatural powers was a way to increase the mystique behind these figures. It also goes back to the idea of disguise: one disguise a shinobi could undertake was that of a yamabushi. These wilderness-dwelling monks were said to practice an esoteric branch of Buddhism known as Shingon, which made use of hand signs and the like as meditative aids.

Other lore in the shinobi manuals references Wu Xing, the classical Chinese elemental system: wood, fire, earth, metal, and water. Each element had certain character traits associated with it, a different direction, time of day, parts of the body, etc. The different elements also interacted with one another via a nourishing and overcoming cycle, as demonstrated in the diagram above. Shinobi were encouraged, even if they did not fully believe in such things, to learn these associations to better gauge how a given target who did adhere to them might act and exploit certain weaknesses.

4. Weapons and skills Possessed by a Ninja

Ninjitsu has incorporated countless other weapons, but many have been lost because they were never recorded. There are many that people know about even today.

Let’s look a little closer at the primary weapons used in the art of Ninjutsu: Katana: The Sword


The art of sword fighting taught in Ninjutsu falls under Kenjutsu. There are several types of swords used.
The Kata is a long, curved sword with a single-bladed edge. It was used primarily for slashing and cutting, and it was a versatile weapon. Samurai also used the katana, but ninjas often carried a shorter and straighter version called a ninjato.

Wooden swords (Bokkens) or bamboo swords (Shinai) can be found among practising novices. But the most iconic ones are the Katanas. These are curved and thin metal swords for slicing up the opponent

Nodachi is a large and heavy sword that is effective against enemies on horseback. The short counterpart of the Nodachi is the Wakazashi, which is a short sword for attacking nearby enemies

Bo and Jo Staffs: Long a short wooden staffs The stick in general is incorporated under the Bojutsu techniques. Thicker sticks, or Staffs, are effective means of countering swords. These include the Hanbo, Jo, and Bo with lengths of 3, 4, and 6 feet respectively.
Sticks, as in Escrima, are better used as defensive weapons and are made from wood or bamboo.

Tanto: A Shorter Blade


Apart from the sword, there are many blades and knives used in ninjitsu. The dagger or the tanto gained popularity for its compact size which allowed it to be hidden.

The Shuriken: The Throwing Star No weapon is as synonymous with the ninja as the shuriken. These are sharp and compact throwing weapons. Shuriken are small, star-shaped or flat, multi-pointed throwing weapons. They were used primarily to distract or disorient opponents, but could also be used for cutting or puncturing targets. Shuriken are easy to conceal and carry, making them an ideal weapon for ninjas. A board showing 14 different types of bladed throwing weapon, or Shuriken.

The Sai: The Pronged Weapon


This is where Ninjitsu gets more creative. The Sai is a traditional weapon with three prongs and the ninja can use it to block swords and trap the opponent. It is used in pairs whereas the Jutte is used as a singular weapon.

Kusarigama: The Chain and Sickle


Nothing’s more painful than being hit by a chain. The Kusari Fundo takes it a step further and adds weights at its ends. The Kusarigama is the variation that also combines a scythe for maximum damage.

The Rope The rope is a fundamental part of ninjitsu and is covered in Hojojutsu. The rope is mainly used to trap and disarm opponents. Rope-based weapons include the Kyoketsu-Shoge, which also uses a dart. Other than these, ninjitsu incorporates many techniques that are used to slow down opponents or blind them temporarily. Ninjas have used them to escape for centuries.

Tekko-Kagi: Hand Claws


Tekko-Kagi are metal bands with sharpened spikes worn on the hands. They were used primarily for climbing, but could also be employed as a weapon for slashing or grappling in close combat. These were made famous by the epic fight scene in American Ninja between Michale Dudikoff’s heroic Ninja, and the dastardly Black Star Ninja. The scene below also has every possible type of Ninja weapon plus some dubious others like Lasers!

Caltrops or Tetsubishi Caltrops are small, four-pointed spikes that were scattered on the ground to impede or injure pursuers. Ninjas would use these to slow down enemies, damage their feet, or even puncture the hooves of pursuing horses.

Metsubshi: Blinding Powders Metsubushi were small containers filled with blinding powder, such as ground pepper or ashes. Ninjas would throw these at their opponents to create a smokescreen, disorient enemies, or facilitate a quick escape.

Fukiya: The Blow-Gun

The blowgun was a simple, lightweight, and easily concealed weapon used by ninjas for shooting poisoned darts or needles from a distance. It was an effective tool for silent assassinations and likely was used in non-combat situations.

Kunai: The Dagger type Tool


A kunai is a multipurpose tool resembling a wide, short-bladed dagger. It was used for digging, prying, and climbing, but could also be used as a weapon in close-quarter combat or as a makeshift throwing weapon.
